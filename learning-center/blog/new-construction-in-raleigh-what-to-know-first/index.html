New Construction

New construction can be amazing. Clean floor plans, modern finishes, and that "brand new" feeling. But if you only shop the model home and the base price, you can get surprised fast. This guide breaks down what we see behind the scenes all the time.

1) Myth: New Means Perfect

In the transcript, we call this out directly. We have seen newer homes with water leaks, HVAC issues, and other punch-list items because production timelines can move fast. Faster timelines can mean more mistakes.

Action: Always do an independent inspection. Not a builder-only walkthrough. Your inspector, your report, your leverage.

2) Know Who the Builder Rep Works For

Builder reps are usually great people, but their job is to represent the builder. That is why independent representation matters, especially when you are negotiating deposits, upgrade credits, or timeline terms.

The transcript highlights deposits that can range from five figures up to much more, depending on price point and community. Terms matter. Deadlines matter. Contract language matters.

3) Base Price Is Not Your Final Price

This is the trap most relocators fall into first. You see a base number online, then walk the model and realize the finishes you like are not in that base package. Cabinets, counters, appliances, lot premiums, backyard improvements, and porch upgrades can add up quickly.

  • Ask for a written "as-built" estimate, not only the base sheet.
  • Price your must-have upgrades before you emotionally commit.
  • Budget for post-closing items like fencing, blinds, and landscaping.

4) Builder Incentives Can Help, But Read the Full Math

Rate buydowns and closing-cost incentives can be excellent tools. But the transcript makes one point very clearly: run the comparison. Sometimes incentive value is offset elsewhere in the transaction.

Action: Get a second lender quote and compare total cost over time, not just the headline monthly payment.

5) HOA Rules and Fee Changes Are Not a Side Note

Many new communities have HOA structures with restrictions and evolving fee schedules as amenities are completed. Ask for the covenants early and read the parts that impact your day-to-day life: parking, exterior modifications, rentals, and projected fee shifts.

6) Delays Happen. Build a Plan B.

Construction timelines can shift due to labor, weather, and material constraints. If your lease, school calendar, or relocation package depends on a specific closing window, build backup options now, not later.

Bottom line: new construction can be a smart move in Raleigh, but only when you protect yourself on inspections, numbers, rules, and timeline risk before you sign.

At A Glance

Bring this checklist to your first builder appointment:

  • Independent inspection plan
  • Line-item upgrade and lot premium budget
  • Lender comparison outside builder financing
  • HOA rules plus long-term fee assumptions
  • Delay contingency and move-in backup plan
